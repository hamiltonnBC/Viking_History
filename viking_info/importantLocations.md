Scandinavia (The Homeland)
Denmark: The origin of many major Viking fleets, including a 200-ship fleet that attacked Frisia in 810 (pg. 71)
. It was ruled by King Cnut the Great, who eventually conquered England in 1016, making Denmark the center of a powerful northern European empire (pg. 56)
. It was home to Hedeby, a critical and wealthy trade town (pg. 2, 74)
, and Lejre on the island of Zealand, which featured the largest known early medieval Scandinavian hall (pg. 7)
.
Norway: Renowned for its long, protected coastal navigation routes (pg. 84)
. Norway is the site of famous archaeological ship burials, such as the Gokstad ship (pg. 30, 74)
 and the Oseberg ship (buried around 815–820), where two women were interred with exceptionally rich grave goods (pg. 7, 92-93)
.
Sweden: The location of the trade town Birka (pg. 2)
 and Gamla Uppsala, which hosted a great chieftain's hall (pg. 64)
. The island of Gotland in the Baltic Sea was a key naval hub; it features numerous picture stones depicting sailing ships and hosted a trading community and shipyard at Paviken (pg. 80, 88-89)
. Valsgärde, located north of Uppsala, is a major burial site containing fifteen ship burials spanning from the late 6th century to roughly the year 1000 (pg. 94)
.
The British Isles & Ireland
England (Northumbria, East Anglia, Wessex): A primary target for Viking raids and eventual conquest. The island monastery of Lindisfarne in Northumbria suffered the first known major Viking raid in 793 (pg. 21)
. The Viking chieftain Halvdan conquered Northumbria in 876, establishing settlements, while Guthrum conquered East Anglia, establishing the "Danelaw"—a vast region where Scandinavian laws and culture took root (pg. 52-53)
. York (Jórvík) served as an important royal seat for Norse kings in Northumbria (pg. 54)
. Maldon in Essex was the site of a famous battle in 991, which led to the English paying massive tributes known as Danegeld (pg. 27-28, 40)
.
Ireland (Dublin): In 917, the Viking Sitric arrived with a fleet and founded the city of Dublin on the Liffey River, which rapidly grew into Ireland's first major commercial center (pg. 54)
. Scandinavian kings ruled Dublin for decades, including Sitric Silkenbeard, who maintained power even after fighting the massive Battle of Clontarf in 1014 against the Irish high king Brian Boru (pg. 55-56)
.
Orkneys, Shetlands, and Hebrides: Island groups heavily settled by Scandinavians. The Norse dialect "Norn" was spoken here into the 19th century, and the region retains a massive genetic and linguistic heritage from Viking Age settlers (pg. 57, 60)
.
Western Continental Europe
Frisia (Modern Netherlands/Belgium): A coastal region repeatedly attacked by Vikings, such as in 810 (pg. 71)
. The declining but wealthy trade town of Dorestad was occupied in 850 by the Viking chieftain Roric, who went on to rule a large portion of Frisia as a vassal for Frankish emperors until his death (pg. 47-48)
.
The Frankish Empire (France/Germany): The rich, undefended cities and monasteries along its rivers were heavily targeted. The city of Nantes on the Loire River was brutally massacred on St. John's Day in 843 (pg. 15-16)
. Paris and Rouen on the Seine River were attacked in 845, during which Vikings captured and executed 111 Frankish soldiers (pg. 22-23)
.
Normandy: A coastal region in modern France that eventually saw heavy Scandinavian presence and utilization of its waterways, such as a Viking fleet stopping in Jumièges in 862 to repair their ships (pg. 80)
.
Eastern Europe, Russia, and Asia
Russia (Novgorod and River Routes): In the early 9th century, the Varangian chieftain Rurik traveled across the Baltic Sea and took up residence in Novgorod, founding the Rurikid dynasty that ruled Russia until the 16th century (pg. 49)
. The Dnieper and Volga Rivers served as critical transportation routes to the East (pg. 80)
. Gnëzdovo (near Smolensk) served as a major transport hub and shipyard at the crossroads of these routes (pg. 80)
.
Bulghar (on the Volga River): A major trading center where the Arab traveler Ahmad ibn Fadlan encountered Rus merchants in 921 and documented a spectacular ship-cremation funeral for a Rus chieftain (pg. 94-96)
.
Byzantine Empire (Constantinople / "Greece"): Scandinavians traveled here to negotiate trade treaties (e.g., in 907) and to serve as highly paid elite mercenary soldiers in the emperor's Varangian Guard (pg. 24, 50)
.
The Caspian Sea & Serkland (Central Asia/Middle East): In the early 11th century, the Swedish chieftain Ingvar led a rare, massive raid down the Russian rivers to the shores of the Caspian Sea and Serkland (the land of the Saracens), ending in disaster for the Vikings (pg. 82)
. Central Asia/India is also notable as the source of the high-carbon steel used to forge the legendary "Ulfberht" swords (pg. 32)
.
The North Atlantic and North America
Iceland: An island fully colonized by Scandinavian settlers, who rapidly cut down its native forests to build homes and ships, prompting them to seek resources further west (pg. 67)
.
Greenland: Settled toward the end of the 10th century by Erik the Red, who built his farm at Brattahlid in the Eastern Settlement (pg. 60-61)
. The island had a Western Settlement which featured sites like the "Farm Beneath the Sand" (pg. 64)
. The Norse here survived by farming and exporting arctic goods like walrus ivory, but the settlements mysteriously died out by the 15th century, with the final written record being a wedding at Hvalsey in 1408 (pg. 65-66)
.
North America (Vinland and Markland): Discovered around the year 1000 by the Greenland explorer Leif Eriksson (pg. 68)
. "Vinland" (Newfoundland) was the site of a temporary Norse settlement at L’Anse aux Meadows, proving European arrival in America five centuries before Columbus (pg. 68-69)
. "Markland" (likely the forested Labrador Peninsula) was used as a vital source of timber for the Greenlanders (pg. 68)
.
