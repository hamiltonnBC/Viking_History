Eastern River and Overland Trade Routes
What it was: A vast network of riverways and overland paths crossing Eastern Europe and Eurasia that allowed ships to traverse the continent through portaging (dragging boats overland between rivers) (Winroth, pg. 9), (Winroth, pg. 80).
From where to where: From the Baltic Sea down the rivers of Russia (such as the Dnieper River starting from Smolensk) to Central Asia, the Arab Caliphate, and connecting via the Silk Road to China (Winroth, pg. 9), (Winroth, pg. 81). Expeditions also traveled as far as the shores of the Caspian Sea (Winroth, pg. 82).
When: Throughout the Viking Age, with specific examples like Rurik's arrival in Novgorod in the early 9th century, Byzantine accounts of the Rus navigating the Dnieper in the 10th century, and the chieftain Ingvar’s expedition to the Caspian Sea in the early 11th century (Winroth, pg. 49), (Winroth, pg. 80), (Winroth, pg. 82).
Why: To amass fortunes as traders and merchants by bringing extraordinary wealth back to the North, including Arab silver, Chinese silk, Indian gems, and the high-quality Asian steel used for forging Ulfberht swords (Winroth, pg. 10), (Winroth, pg. 32). They were also used to extract tribute and conduct raids (Winroth, pg. 82).
From who: Wealth and exotic goods were acquired from Arab, Asian, and Byzantine merchants, while tribute was extracted from indigenous Eastern European peoples like the Slavs, Chuds, and Krivichians (Winroth, pg. 10), (Winroth, pg. 49).
Used by who: Scandinavian merchants, Varangians, the Rus, and Viking chieftains (such as Rurik and Ingvar) (Winroth, pg. 49), (Winroth, pg. 82).
Western Raid and Coastal Routes
What it was: Fast, open-water sea voyages and deep river incursions utilized to perform sudden, unheralded attacks on undefended towns and monasteries (Winroth, pg. 11), (Winroth, pg. 18).
From where to where: From Scandinavia (or occupied island bases like Noirmoutier) across the North Sea or Bay of Biscay to the coasts and rivers of the Frankish Empire and the British Isles (Winroth, pg. 16), (Winroth, pg. 77). Fleets navigated up the Loire to Nantes, the Seine to Rouen and Paris, the Garonne to Toulouse, the Thames in England, and the Rhine in Frisia (Winroth, pg. 15), (Winroth, pg. 20), (Winroth, pg. 22), (Winroth, pg. 72).
When: From the late 8th century (e.g., Lindisfarne in 793) up through the 11th century (e.g., Cnut's conquests in 1016) (Winroth, pg. 21), (Winroth, pg. 56).
Why: Initially to acquire wealth through plunder (gold, silver, and human captives) and to extort massive tribute payments known as Danegeld (Winroth, pg. 18), (Winroth, pg. 40). Later, ambitious chieftains used these routes to conquer territory and establish settlements, such as the Danelaw in England or domains in Frisia and Normandy (Winroth, pg. 48), (Winroth, pg. 53).
From who: Booty, tribute, and land were forcibly taken from the inhabitants of the Frankish Empire, independent duchies, and the British Isles (e.g., Franks, Anglo-Saxons, Irish, and Frisians) (Winroth, pg. 16), (Winroth, pg. 40).
Used by who: Viking warrior bands and powerful chieftains such as Roric, Ragnar Hairy-Breeches, Halvdan, Guthrum, and Cnut (Winroth, pg. 34), (Winroth, pg. 47), (Winroth, pg. 52).
Transatlantic Settlement and Exploration Routes
What it was: Open-ocean navigational routes crossing the North Atlantic without the use of modern instruments, navigated by observing natural clues like cloud formations, sea currents, bird species, and whales (Winroth, pg. 85).
From where to where: From the western coast of Norway to the Faroe Islands, Iceland, Greenland, and onward to North America, passing Markland (likely the forested Labrador peninsula) to reach Vinland (Newfoundland) (Winroth, pg. 68), (Winroth, pg. 85).
When: Late 10th century (Greenland was settled toward the end of the 10th century) and around the year 1000 (discovery of Vinland) (Winroth, pg. 60), (Winroth, pg. 68). As the climate worsened starting in the 13th century, expanding ice masses forced sailors to find new sailing routes between Greenland and Iceland (Winroth, pg. 66).
Why: For emigration and colonization; chieftains and farmers pursued agriculture and animal husbandry to carve out new lives away from the power struggles of Scandinavia (Winroth, pg. 51), (Winroth, pg. 60). Expeditions to North America were specifically driven by Greenland's lack of timber, prompting sailors to seek out wood for building homes and ships, as well as new hunting grounds (Winroth, pg. 67-68).
From who: Navigational knowledge was passed down from generation to generation. While areas like Iceland were mostly uninhabited, travelers further west interacted with, traded with, and occasionally fought indigenous populations, such as the Inuit in Greenland and the people the Norse called "skrælingar" in North America (Winroth, pg. 66), (Winroth, pg. 68), (Winroth, pg. 84).
Used by who: Scandinavian emigrants, farmers, and chieftains seeking fresh starts, such as Erik the Red and his son Leif Eriksson (Winroth, pg. 60), (Winroth, pg. 68).
Scandinavian Coastal and Archipelago Navigation Routes
What it was: Domestic, protected navigation routes that allowed ships to utilize localized archipelagos to safely travel without entering the volatile open ocean (Winroth, pg. 84).
From where to where: Through the thousands of islands stretching from eastern Scania to the Gulf of Finland in the Baltic Sea, and along the long western coast of Norway (e.g., from the far North down to the Oslo Fjord) (Winroth, pg. 84).
When: Used throughout the Viking Age, with a specific documented example of the merchant Ottar utilizing them in the late 9th century (Winroth, pg. 84).
Why: To facilitate safe communication, trade, and travel. Ships could "sail by the coast the whole way," remaining close to land to easily avoid storms and find safe places to camp at night (Winroth, pg. 84).
From who: The knowledge of these routes and landmarks was passed down from previous generations of seafarers (Winroth, pg. 84).
Used by who: Everyday Scandinavian seafarers, traveling chieftains, and merchants such as Ottar (Winroth, pg. 84).
