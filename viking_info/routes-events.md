Eastern River and Overland Trade Routes
Early 9th Century (Rurik's Settlement): Indigenous peoples like the Chuds, Slavs, and Krivichians invited Varangians to rule them, resulting in the chieftain Rurik taking up residence in Novgorod and founding the Rurikid dynasty (pg. 49)
.
907 (Treaty with Constantinople): A Rus delegation featuring Scandinavian names like Karl, Velmud, and Rulav traveled to Constantinople to negotiate a trade treaty with the Byzantine Empire (pg. 50)
.
921 (Ibn Fadlan's Observation): Arab traveler Ahmad ibn Fadlan encountered Rus merchants in the town of Bulghar on the Volga River and witnessed the magnificent ship-cremation of a Rus chieftain (pg. 94)
.
10th Century (Dnieper River Navigation): Byzantine Emperor Constantine VII documented how the Rus traveled from Smolensk down the Dnieper River, dragging or carrying their dugout canoes overland to bypass dangerous rapids like the "Aifor" cataract to reach the Black Sea (pg. 80-81)
.
Early 11th Century (Ingvar's Caspian Expedition): The Swedish chieftain Ingvar led a large Viking fleet—including men like Banki who steered his own ship—on a rare expedition to the shores of the Caspian Sea, though the expedition failed and most of the men perished after meeting strong resistance (pg. 82)
,
.
Western Raid and Coastal Routes
793 (Raid on Lindisfarne): The first known major Viking raid on a monastery occurred at Lindisfarne in northeastern England, prompting the theologian Alcuin to view the attack as an eschatological event (pg. 21)
.
810 (Fleet in Frisia): A fleet of 200 Danish ships landed in Frisia, fought three battles, and extorted a massive ransom before sailing away just as Emperor Charlemagne marched north to confront them (pg. 71)
.
843 (Massacre at Nantes): On St. John's Day, a fleet sailed up the Loire River and suddenly attacked the city of Nantes, slaughtering the local bishop at the altar of his cathedral and hauling away captives (pg. 15-16)
,
.
845 (Attack on Paris): Vikings navigated the Seine River without opposition, plundered Rouen, and captured 111 Frankish soldiers whom they hanged in plain sight to terrify King Charles the Bald and his army (pg. 22-23)
.
850 (Occupation of Dorestad): The Danish Viking Roric attacked the declining but wealthy trade town of Dorestad, successfully occupying it and forcing Emperor Lothar to accept him as a vassal ruling over a large portion of Frisia (pg. 47)
.
859 (Mediterranean Incursion): Danish pirates sailed through the straits between Spain and Africa and navigated up the Rhone River to ravage monasteries and cities (pg. 72)
.
876 (Conquest of Northumbria): The Viking chieftain Halvdan successfully conquered Northumbria in England and divided the land among his warriors so they could settle and farm (pg. 52)
.
878 (Guthrum's Baptism): After conquering East Anglia, the Viking leader Guthrum assimilated into the local culture, accepted baptism with King Alfred the Great as his godfather, and took the Anglo-Saxon name Ethelstan (pg. 52)
.
890s (Hæsten's Invasion): A large Viking army led by Hæsten established a fortification at Benfleet in Essex, bringing women and children with them, until the English captured the fort and Hæsten's family in 893 (pg. 57)
.
917 (Founding of Dublin): The brothers Ragnall and Sitric arrived in Ireland with Viking fleets; Sitric took control of a settlement on the Liffey River, building a fortress that laid the foundation for the city of Dublin (pg. 54)
.
991 (Battle of Maldon): Vikings demanded a tribute of gold rings, leading to a battle where they defeated the English forces and killed Ealdorman Byrhtnoth, which resulted in the first large Danegeld payment of 10,000 pounds of silver (pg. 27, 40)
,
.
1014 (Battle of Clontarf): The Scandinavian ruler of Dublin, Sitric Silkenbeard, fought a massive battle just north of the city against the Irish high king Brian Boru, resulting in an Irish victory but allowing Sitric to remain in power for decades (pg. 55)
,
.
1016 (Conquest of England): The Danish king Cnut successfully conquered England in its entirety, becoming one of the most powerful rulers in northern Europe (pg. 56)
.
Transatlantic Settlement and Exploration Routes
Late 10th Century (Settlement of Greenland): Erik the Red led an expedition to settle Greenland, establishing his chieftain's hall at Brattahlid in the Eastern Settlement (pg. 60-61)
,
.
Around 1000 (Discovery of Vinland): Erik the Red's son, Leif Eriksson, explored further west, passing forested Markland (the Labrador peninsula) and establishing a brief settlement in Vinland (Newfoundland), which was eventually abandoned due to hostile encounters with indigenous populations (pg. 68)
,
.
Early 11th Century (The Farm Beneath the Sand): Settlers established a farm in Greenland's Western Settlement featuring a small turf hall, which was later converted to house sheep for wool production (pg. 64)
.
1126 (Establishment of a Bishopric): Norse Greenland had grown to support over a dozen parish churches and was granted its own bishop, who resided in Gardar (pg. 62)
.
1350s (Abandonment of the Western Settlement): The priest Ivar Bardarson traveled to the Western Settlement and discovered it entirely abandoned by people, leaving only half-wild farm animals behind (pg. 65)
.
1408 (Last Written Record of Greenland Norse): A young couple, Sigrid Björnsdottir and Thorstein Olafsson, were married in the church at Hvalsey; this is the final written record of the Norse in Greenland before they mysteriously vanished from the island (pg. 66)
.
Scandinavian Coastal and Archipelago Navigation Routes
Late 9th Century (Ottar's Travels): The chieftain and merchant Ottar regularly sailed from his home in the far north of Norway down to a trading hub in the Oslo Fjord (pg. 84)
. He successfully navigated the long western coast by camping at night and using familiar landmarks (pg. 84)
.
1000 (Battle of Svöldr): A great naval clash where the Norwegian King Olav Tryggvason fought against a coalition of Swedish and Danish kings and fell in battle (pg. 88)
.
1016 (Battle of Nesjar): A major sea battle where Olav Haraldsson won the Norwegian kingship (pg. 88)
.
1062 (Battle of Nissan River): King Harald Hardruler of Norway fought the Danish King Svein Estridsson on the water; the Norwegians won the battle, but Svein escaped in a small rowboat (pg. 88)
