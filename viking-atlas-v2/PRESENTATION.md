# Viking Atlas Presentation Script

## Opening (The Big Picture)

The Viking Age spans roughly 750 to 1100 AD, with its aftereffects lingering until 1408. It begins with Scandinavian warriors appearing without warning on the coasts of Europe, and ends with their descendants ruling kingdoms, founding cities, and vanishing into the frozen edges of the known world.

The people we call "Vikings" were not a single nation. They were Danes, Norwegians, and Swedes, united by language, shipbuilding technology, and a shared culture of seafaring. What made them extraordinary was not just their violence, but their reach. In three centuries, they raided Paris, traded in Baghdad, settled Iceland, and reached North America, five hundred years before Columbus.

This application maps that entire story geographically and chronologically.

## The Four Eras

### Age of Raids (750–869)

This is where it starts. The raid on the monastery at Lindisfarne in 793 is traditionally considered the opening act of the Viking Age. Norse warriors discovered that the wealthy, undefended monasteries and trading towns of Christian Europe were easy targets. Their ships could appear without warning, strike fast, and vanish before any army could respond.

Key events to show on the map:
- Lindisfarne (793), the first major raid
- Danish fleet of 200 ships hitting Frisia (810)
- The sack of Nantes on St. John's Day (843), timed to catch the city full of pilgrims
- The siege of Paris (845), where Vikings hanged 111 captives in plain view to terrify the Frankish king
- A Danish fleet of 62 ships passing through the Straits of Gibraltar into the Mediterranean (859)

The raids weren't random. They followed specific routes, which you can see on the map: across the North Sea to England, down the Channel to France, through the Baltic to the east.

### Age of Conquest & Settlement (870–979)

The raiders became settlers. Instead of taking treasure and leaving, they started taking land and staying. In England, the chieftain Halvdan carved up Northumbria and distributed farms to his warriors in 876. The Danelaw, a vast region of Norse law and culture, was established across eastern England.

In Ireland, the Norse founded Dublin in 917, Ireland's first true city. In France, the Viking chieftain Rollo was granted Normandy in 911.

Meanwhile, Norwegian emigrants were pushing west across the Atlantic. Iceland was settled around 874. By the late 900s, Erik the Red had established colonies in Greenland.

On the eastern routes, Swedish Vikings known as the Varangians or Rus were navigating Russia's river systems, founding Novgorod, and signing trade treaties with Constantinople.

### Age of Kings (980–1100)

This is the peak. Around the year 1000, Leif Eriksson reached North America from Greenland. At the same time, Cnut of Denmark conquered England in 1016 and ruled a North Sea empire encompassing England, Denmark, and Norway.

The great naval battles of this era, Svöldr (1000), Nesjar (1016), and the Nissan River (1062), show that control of Scandinavian waters was the key to political power.

The era ends with the Battle of Hastings in 1066, where William the Conqueror, a descendant of the Viking Rollo, defeats the English king. The Viking Age closes with Vikings on both sides of the battlefield.

### End of the Viking Age (1101–1408)

The Norse world contracts. Greenland received its own bishop in 1126, showing it was still a functioning Christian community. But the climate worsened, ice expanded, and the settlements slowly faded. The last written record of Norse Greenland is a wedding at Hvalsey church in 1408. After that, silence.

## The Routes (What Made It All Possible)

The map shows four types of routes:

**Raid Routes (red):** The corridors of violence. From Denmark across the North Sea to England. Down the Channel and up the Seine to Paris. Through the Loire to Nantes. Around Scotland to Ireland. Even through Gibraltar into the Mediterranean. These routes followed coastlines and rivers, allowing raiders to strike deep inland.

**Trade Routes (gold):** The corridors of commerce. The Dnieper route ran from Sweden through the Gulf of Finland, down through Russia to Constantinople. The Volga route went to the Caspian Sea and the Arab Caliphate. Merchants carried furs, slaves, and amber south in exchange for Arab silver, Chinese silk, and Indian gems. The Scandinavian Coastal Route connected Norwegian and Baltic ports through sheltered archipelagos.

**Exploration Routes (teal):** The corridors of discovery. From western Norway to the Faroe Islands, Iceland, Greenland, and ultimately Newfoundland. These were open-ocean crossings navigated without instruments, using clouds, currents, birds, and whales as guides.

## The Locations

**Scandinavia (the homelands):**
- Denmark: the fleet-launching heartland, home to Hedeby and the royal seat at Jelling
- Western Norway: the Atlantic departure point for all westward voyages
- Eastern Norway: the Oslo Fjord, where the great ship burials (Oseberg, Gokstad) were interred
- Sweden: Birka, Gotland, and the gateway to the eastern river routes

**The targets and settlements:**
- England: raided from 793, conquered by 1016. York became a Norse royal seat.
- France: Paris besieged, Nantes sacked, Normandy eventually granted to the Norse
- Ireland: Dublin founded as the first Irish city, ruled by Norse kings for decades
- Frisia: repeatedly raided, Dorestad occupied

**The eastern network:**
- Rus Lands: Novgorod, the Dnieper and Volga rivers, trade with Constantinople and Baghdad

**The Atlantic frontier:**
- Iceland: settled 874, became a republic with its own parliament
- Greenland: settled by Erik the Red, lasted until the 1400s
- Vinland: Leif Eriksson reached Newfoundland around 1000 AD

## The Ships

Everything depended on the ships. The longship was the technology that made the Viking Age possible. It could cross open oceans, navigate shallow rivers, and be dragged overland between waterways. The map shows ship-related events: the Oseberg burial (834), the Gokstad burial (900), the Hedeby warship (985), the Roskilde Giant (1025), and Ibn Fadlan's account of a ship cremation on the Volga (921).

---

## Closing

The Viking Age lasted roughly three centuries, but its effects shaped the modern world. The Norse founded Dublin, established Normandy (whose duke would conquer England), created the Russian state, reached America, and connected Scandinavia to Baghdad through trade. They were raiders, traders, settlers, and explorers, and this map traces all of it from the first raid in 793 to the last wedding in Greenland in 1408.
