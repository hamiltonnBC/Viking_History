## **Winroth, Anders. *The Age of the Vikings*. Princeton UP, 2016\.**

## **Timeline: Key Dates & Historical Eras**

**774: Charlemagne’s Conquest of Pavia and the Lombard Treasury.** Charlemagne, King of the Franks, conquered northern Italy and seized the royal treasury of the Lombards. This event is a critical precursor to the Viking Age because it demonstrates that successful early medieval kings relied on a "gift economy" fueled by seized booty to reward magnates and maintain the loyalty of their followers—the same system Viking chieftains would later utilize. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**782: The Massacre of Verden.** Charlemagne ordered the decapitation of 4,500 Saxons in a single day as punishment for their resistance and oath-breaking. This act of state-sponsored violence puts Viking raids into perspective, showing that the contemporary Christian world was often more brutal and destructive than the Scandinavian raiders. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

c. 800: The Adoption of the Sail. Around this time, picture stones on the island of Gotland began to depict ships with sails rather than just rowing boats. This technological shift was the "great mystery" of Scandinavian history, as it took centuries for the Norse to adopt Roman-style sailing technology, but once they did, it allowed for the long-distance ocean travel required for the Viking Age. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 4**

**Early 9th Century: Varangian Tribute in Russia.** Viking bands, known as Varangians, began forcing indigenous peoples like the Chuds and Slavs to pay tribute to stop their raiding. This established a pattern of Norse dominance in northwestern Russia that eventually led to the invitation of Rurik to rule. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 3**

**793: The Raid on Lindisfarne.** Vikings plundered the island monastery of Lindisfarne in northeastern England, an event often cited as the definitive start of the Viking Age. It was historically significant for the theological shock it sent through Europe. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**796: The Looting of the "Avar Ring."** Charlemagne’s army defeated the Avars in Hungary and discovered undreamed-of riches. This massive influx of wealth was distributed among Charlemagne's followers, illustrating the essential need for rulers to acquire loot or tribute to ensure the survival of their kingdom. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**810: The Danish Fleet Challenges Charlemagne.** A fleet of 200 Danish ships ravaged Frisia, forcing the locals to pay a large ransom. This event is historically significant because it demonstrated how the speed and shallow draught of Viking ships allowed them to appear suddenly and disappear before the slow, regular Frankish army could engage them. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 4**

**815–820: Construction of the Oseberg Ship.** Built in Norway, this is the oldest Scandinavian sailing ship found by archeologists. Its design shows the early, somewhat awkward transition to sail technology, featuring a mast that was likely unsafe in high winds, yet it remains one of the most exquisitely carved examples of Viking naval art. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 4**

**834 (c.): The Oseberg Ship Burial.** Two high-status women were buried in the Oseberg ship with incredibly rich grave goods, including a wagon, sleds, and slaughtered horses. This burial illustrates the Norse belief that the Afterworld was reached by ship, with the vessel functioning as a "ferry" to the realm of the dead. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 4**

**836–837: The Devastation of Frisia and Dorestad.** Viking fleets began consistent, "usual" surprise attacks on the Frisian coast and the trading hub of Dorestad. These raids were tactically important because they established the "warfare by incursion" style. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**840–843: The Frankish Civil War.** Following the death of Emperor Louis the Pious, his three sons tore the empire apart in a bloody civil war. This period is crucial because it created a military power vacuum that Vikings used their fast ships to exploit. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**June 24, 843: The Sack of Nantes.** Vikings attacked Nantes on St. John’s Day, a major Christian feast when the city was full of people and wealth. This was a highly strategic strike based on intelligence. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**844: The Plunder of Dún Masc.** Irish annals recorded the destruction of Dún Masc, where church officials like the abbot of Tír dá Glas were killed. These records show the geographic reach of the "heathen" raids across Ireland. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**845: The Siege of Paris and Psychological Warfare.** During an attack on Paris, Vikings captured 111 Frankish soldiers and hanged them in plain sight of King Charles the Bald’s army. This act of psychological warfare was intended to demoralize the professional Frankish soldiers. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 2**

**850: Roric Captures Dorestad.** The Viking leader Roric took the major trade town of Dorestad and refused to leave. Realizing he could not be easily expelled, Emperor Lothar accepted Roric as his vassal, essentially hiring a "Viking to fight Vikings" to defend the region. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 3**

**851: The Massive Fleet in the Thames.** The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle recorded that 350 ships entered the mouth of the Thames. This highlights the massive scale that Viking expeditions could reach by the mid-9th century. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 4**

**859: The Mediterranean Raid.** Danish pirates sailed through the Straits of Gibraltar and up the Rhone River to ravage cities and monasteries. This expedition proved the incredible range of Viking ships, which could navigate both the open ocean and shallow inland rivers. **Source: The Age of the Vikings, Chapter 4**
